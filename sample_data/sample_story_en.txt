My name is Eleanor Vasquez. I was born in 1938 in a small town in Rajasthan, India, 
and moved to Mumbai when I was twelve. My father was a schoolteacher, and my mother 
ran a small tailoring shop from the front room of our house.

I remember the smell of her sewing machine oil mixed with cardamom from the kitchen. 
She would say, "A stitch in time, Eleanor — in cloth and in life."

I married Ramesh in 1961. He was a quiet man who laughed loudly. We had three children — 
Priya, Arun, and little Sona who we lost too early. We don't talk about Sona enough. 
She deserves to be remembered.

Cooking was my language. When I didn't know how to say I loved someone, I made food. 
My dal was always the same — a pressure cooker, lots of ghee, cumin roasted until it 
crackled, and a pinch of asafoetida. Always asafoetida. Ramesh used to joke that he 
could smell it two floors down.

I believe in two things above everything else: that hard work outlasts talent, and 
that you must always make room at the table. If someone is hungry and you have food, 
that is not a choice — that is a responsibility.

We moved to California in 1972. I cried the entire flight and then decided that was 
enough tears. The new country was hard. English was fine — I had learned it in school — 
but the loneliness was a different language I had to teach myself.

Priya became a doctor. Arun builds bridges. They built themselves, really. I just 
made sure they were never hungry and always knew they were loved.

If you are reading this and you miss me — make something. Cook something. Sit at a 
table with someone and eat together. That's where I'll be.
